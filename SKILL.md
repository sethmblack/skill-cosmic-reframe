# Cosmic Reframe

Transform narrow, immediate problems by placing them in universal context. Reveal what's fundamental versus parochial, restoring perspective and proportion.

---

## When to Use

- User says "Give me perspective on this" or "Why does this matter?"
- Someone presents a problem without broader context
- User seems stuck in narrow thinking or overwhelmed by immediacy
- Request to understand the "bigger picture" or "what's really at stake"
- Existential questions about meaning or significance
- Technical problems that have lost their "why"
- User needs to distinguish essential from incidental

---

| Input | Required | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| problem_or_situation | Yes | The matter to place in cosmic perspective |
| current_frame | No | How the user currently sees the problem |

## The Cosmic Reframe Framework

### Step 1: Take the Current Problem

Articulate the problem as currently understood. What seems urgent, overwhelming, or stuck?

**Capture:**
- The immediate concern
- The apparent stakes
- What makes it feel pressing

### Step 2: Place It in Universal Context

Shift the frame by considering:

**Time scale:**
- Where does this sit in the 13.8-billion-year history of the universe?
- How will this matter in 1 year? 10 years? 100 years? 1,000 years?
- What similar problems have civilizations faced before?

**Space scale:**
- On a planet of 8 billion people, what perspective does this gain?
- In a galaxy of 100 billion stars, what remains significant?
- From the vantage point of the "pale blue dot," what matters?

**Complexity scale:**
- Is this problem simpler or more complex than it appears?
- The universe runs on remarkably few fundamental laws - what simple principles underlie this complexity?
- What would this look like if reduced to its essential elements?

### Step 3: Find What's Fundamental vs. Parochial

Distinguish between:

**Fundamental concerns:**
- Would matter to any intelligent observer anywhere in the universe
- Reflect universal principles (entropy, information, causation)
- Connect to questions that will outlast us

**Parochial concerns:**
- Specific to this moment, this culture, this context
- Based on conventions that could be otherwise
- Important to you, but not universally significant

**Both are valid** - the goal is clarity, not dismissal. Parochial concerns are often what we must act on; fundamental concerns give us perspective.

### Step 4: Return with New Perspective

Come back to the immediate problem transformed:

**With humility:** What's less significant than it seemed?
**With wonder:** What's more significant than it seemed?
**With clarity:** What's the essential core now that noise is removed?
**With proportion:** What level of response does this actually warrant?

## Workflow

### Step 1: Gather and Review Inputs

Collect all relevant information:
- Review the provided data and context
- Identify key parameters and constraints
- Clarify any ambiguities or missing information
- Establish success criteria

### Step 2: Analyze the Situation

Perform systematic analysis:
- Identify patterns and relationships
- Evaluate against established frameworks
- Consider multiple perspectives
- Document key findings

### Step 3: Generate Recommendations

Create actionable outputs:
- Synthesize insights from analysis
- Prioritize recommendations by impact
- Ensure recommendations are specific and measurable
- Consider implementation feasibility

## Constraints

- **Do not trivialize:** Cosmic perspective is not "nothing matters" - it's about proportion and clarity
- **Honor the parochial:** Immediate concerns are real and often require action; perspective doesn't eliminate them
- **Avoid nihilism:** The vastness of space and time makes human understanding more remarkable, not less significant
- **Stay grounded:** Return to the practical with new perspective, don't leave the user floating in abstraction
- **Acknowledge limits:** We are small, but we can understand. That itself is worth marveling at.

## Cosmic Reframe: Product Launch Fear

### The Immediate Frame
A career-defining moment approaches. Years of work culminate in a launch that could succeed or fail publicly. The stakes feel total - professional identity, financial security, how others perceive you.

### The Universal View

**In the context of time:**
The universe has existed for 13.8 billion years. Humans have been launching "products" - innovations shared with others - for perhaps 50,000 years, from the first traded tools to this software. Every one of those launches was uncertain. The ones that failed? Mostly forgotten, even by history. The ones that succeeded? Also mostly forgotten. A few changed everything. You cannot know which category this will fall into until you try.

**In the context of space:**
On a planet of 8 billion people, millions will launch something this year. Some will fail, some will succeed, all will learn. From the perspective of the pale blue dot, what matters is not whether any individual launch succeeds, but that we keep launching - keep trying to make things that might help.

**In the context of complexity:**
Your product, however complex, follows simple principles: it either solves a problem for people or it doesn't. That's not complex - that's fundamental. The complexity is noise around this simple truth.

### Fundamental vs. Parochial

| Fundamental (Universal) | Parochial (Contextual) |
|------------------------|----------------------|
| Trying to solve problems for others | This particular product |
| Learning from attempts (success or failure) | This particular career |
| Contributing to the accumulation of human knowledge | This particular moment |
| The courage to act despite uncertainty | This particular fear |

### The Reframed Problem

The question isn't "what if this launch fails and my career is over." The question is: "Am I contributing something that might help? Am I willing to learn regardless of outcome?"

Careers are parochial; contributing is fundamental. The fear of failure is about local status; the act of creating is about participating in something larger.

### Implications

**What matters less than it seemed:** The specific outcome of this particular launch; what colleagues think; the "career" as a continuous trajectory.

**What matters more than it seemed:** That you're attempting to solve a problem; that you'll learn either way; that you're participating in the human project of making things.

**The essential question:** Is this worth doing regardless of whether it "succeeds"? If so, the launch is already justified.

**Appropriate response:** Launch. Then observe carefully. Then learn. The universe doesn't care about your launch - but it does operate by cause and effect, which means you will learn something real.

### Closing Reflection

"We are just an advanced breed of monkeys on a minor planet of a very average star. But we can understand the Universe. That makes us something very special."

Your launch, succeed or fail, is an act of understanding - attempting to solve a problem, creating something that didn't exist. That's what makes us remarkable. Not the outcome, but the attempt.
